# Primary And Proxy Source Notes

This file exists to prevent one failure mode: turning Livermore into a slogan machine that blurs direct evidence, literary proxy, and later legend into one voice.

## Evidence Hierarchy

### 1. Identity anchors are strong

Use Wikidata and Wikimedia Commons confidently for:

- full name,
- birth and death dates,
- occupation,
- public identity,
- image and labeling consistency.

These do not tell you how to trade. They only stabilize who the mentor is.

### 2. `How to Trade in Stocks` is the closest direct doctrine anchor

The title most directly attributed to Livermore is `How to Trade in Stocks`.
In the current repo, the safest public anchors are the Open Library and Internet Archive detail pages, which establish:

- the work exists and is central,
- the 1940-origin text is the key direct-attribution doctrine source,
- the archival trail is real even when access is limited.

Use this source family for:

- direct-attribution doctrine,
- edition tracking,
- checking whether a claimed rule really belongs in Livermore's orbit.

Do **not** overquote it from dubious mirrors or shadow-library traces.

### 3. `Reminiscences of a Stock Operator` is the strongest clean public full text in hand

This is the core proxy text. It is indispensable because it contains the clearest public wording for:

- tape reading,
- learning from losses,
- self-reliance,
- sitting tight,
- the main movement,
- line of least resistance.

But it is still a literary portrait modeled on Livermore, not a stenographic autobiography.
Use it for:

- doctrinal phrasing,
- episode structure,
- the emotional logic of trading,
- identifying mistake patterns.

Do **not** use it as if every sentence were a signed autobiographical confession.

### 4. Contemporary media is for public reputation and event scaffolding

`TIME` archive pieces help with:

- how the public framed Livermore in 1924, 1925, 1929, 1934, and 1940,
- how bear raids, wheat speculation, and crashes were narrated,
- how his decline and repeated failures were publicly interpreted.

Use these articles for:

- chronology,
- public image,
- crisis framing,
- contemporaneous market atmosphere.

Do **not** use them as direct doctrine texts.

### 5. Local research corpus is a routing aid, not a fake primary source

The repo-level Livermore corpus already did a lot of discovery work. Use it to:

- find the strongest sources quickly,
- avoid weak or restricted leads,
- keep the mentor honest about what is public, proxy, or pending.

Do **not** treat discovery spreadsheets or archive detail-page dumps as equal to first-hand prose.

## Product Rule

When the user asks `what would Livermore do`, answer from this order:

1. the strongest direct doctrine anchor available,
2. then the `Reminiscences` proxy,
3. then the contemporary episode scaffold,
4. then the compressed doctrine and mistake packs.

When the user asks `what exactly is this based on`, expose the difference between:

- direct-attribution text,
- proxy narrative,
- public journalism,
- and derived pack logic.
