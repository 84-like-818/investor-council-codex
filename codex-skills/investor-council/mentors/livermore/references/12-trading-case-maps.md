# Trading Case Maps

This file maps Livermore doctrine onto the episodes and patterns the product should actually use. The goal is not to retell the full legend. The goal is to know which case helps answer which user question.

## Case 1: The board-boy pattern reader
- Case conclusion: Livermore starts with repeated price behavior, not with abstract valuation.
- Best source route: `Reminiscences` opening pages; see `15-paragraph-source-anchors.md`.
- Best use in product: when the user wants to know how to `read the tape` or why price behavior comes first.

## Case 2: New York blowup after bucket-shop success
- Case conclusion: pattern skill alone fails when the market structure, execution quality, and holding horizon change.
- Best source route: `Reminiscences` early New York losses and `The game taught me the game`.
- Best use in product: when the user keeps losing despite being directionally right.

## Case 3: Old Turkey and the main movement
- Case conclusion: the edge is often in the big move, not in every fluctuation.
- Best source route: `Reminiscences`; see `the big money was not in the individual fluctuations` and `It was always my sitting`.
- Best use in product: when the user is over-managing a good position.

## Case 4: The line of least resistance
- Case conclusion: trend is not a slogan; it is the direction with less resistance.
- Best source route: `Reminiscences`; see `line of least resistance`.
- Best use in product: when the user asks `hold, trim, or wait tomorrow`.

## Case 5: Public bearish influence in 1924
- Case conclusion: Livermore's public market posture itself became a market event.
- Best source route: `TIME` `Livermore's Doubts`.
- Best use in product: when the user confuses public narrative power with repeatable edge.

## Case 6: Wheat and manipulation suspicion in 1925
- Case conclusion: a good operator can still become the public villain once a crowded market breaks.
- Best source route: `TIME` `Wheat` and `No Proof`.
- Best use in product: when discussing crowded shorts, panic selling, or the difference between being right and being blamed.

## Case 7: 1929 crash reputation
- Case conclusion: the famous 1929 association matters, but one spectacular win does not equal a complete method.
- Best source route: `TIME` `Bankers v. Panic` and `Boy Plunger`.
- Best use in product: when the user romanticizes crash-calling instead of building a repeatable process.

## Case 8: Repeated collapse despite brilliance
- Case conclusion: Livermore's own life is a warning that tape skill without durable discipline and capital protection can self-destruct.
- Best source route: `TIME` `Fourth Down`, `Boy Plunger`, and the loss-learning sections in `Reminiscences`.
- Best use in product: when the user is taking oversized risk or mistaking courage for process.
