# Case Source Anchors

This file gives `12-investment-case-maps.md` a proper evidence layer.
The point is not to retell the story. The point is to make the product know which official materials to revisit when a user asks about a case.

## GEICO
- Case conclusion: low cost, direct acquisition, underwriting discipline, and customer economics together make the insurance moat.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2007ltr.pdf`: GEICO as the widest moat inside Berkshire insurance.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2010ltr.pdf`: 1996 acquisition logic and goodwill explained through customer economics.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/1998ar/1998ar.pdf`: long-run market-share expansion through advertising and cost structure.
- Best return years: 1998, 2007, 2010.
- Product use: use this when the user asks about insurance, acquisition cost, float, or customer lifetime value.

## American Express
- Case conclusion: a true franchise can survive a bad episode without losing the core advantage.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2018ar/2018ar.pdf`: American Express as a major long-held investee in the look-through earnings frame.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/ownman.pdf?v=1694822476156`: owner framing instead of ticker framing.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf`: long-term owner lens rather than hot-money lens.
- Best return years: 2018, 2023.
- Product use: use this when the user asks about temporary shock vs permanent impairment, brand resilience, or payment-network durability.

## Coca-Cola
- Case conclusion: brand, distribution, repeat purchase, and pricing power together create a compounding consumer moat.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2018ar/2018ar.pdf`: Coca-Cola inside the long-term major-holding / look-through frame.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2000-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: early governance and compensation lens.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2014-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: governance boundaries around Coke executive pay.
- Best return years: 2000, 2014, 2018.
- Product use: use this when the user asks about brand moat, governance, or long-duration consumer franchises.

## ISCAR
- Case conclusion: the best acquisitions are not just financial screens; they are combinations of people, culture, product, and customer value.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2006ar/2006ar.pdf`: the Wertheimer letter, trusted management, and why the product matters to customers.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2006-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: meeting language explaining why ISCAR mattered.
- Best return years: 2006.
- Product use: use this when the user asks what a great company plus a great management team looks like in acquisition form.

## BNSF
- Case conclusion: capital intensity does not disqualify a business if it is essential, durable, and understandable.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2009ar/2009ar.pdf`: why BNSF was worth owning as part of the American backbone.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2010ar/2010ar.pdf`: Berkshire after BNSF as a more operating-business-driven group.
- Best return years: 2009, 2010.
- Product use: use this when the user asks about rail, grid, utility, pipeline, or infrastructure-like assets.

## Heinz
- Case conclusion: a good asset can become a better investment when the deal structure is designed well.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2013ar/2013ar.pdf`: Heinz common, preferred, and warrant structure.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2013-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: meeting logic for the Lemann partnership and deal design.
- Best return years: 2013.
- Product use: use this when the user asks about preferreds, warrants, deal design, or risk/reward shaping in acquisitions.

## Apple
- Case conclusion: circle of competence is not a sector label; it is whether Buffett truly understands the business model, ecosystem, and capital return engine.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2018ar/2018ar.pdf`: Apple as a major holding plus book-value and repurchase logic.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2017-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: Apple in, IBM out, and the competence-boundary update.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2023-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: Apple as a better business than any wholly owned Berkshire company.
- Best return years: 2017, 2018, 2023.
- Product use: use this when the user asks why Buffett can own what looks like a tech company without betraying his framework.

## Kraft Heinz / Precision Castparts
- Case conclusion: good assets and strong brands can still turn into poor investments if Buffett overpays, overstates durability, or overestimates fixability.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2019ar/2019ar.pdf`: Kraft Heinz pressure, impairment context, and capital-allocation consequences.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2021ar/2021ar.pdf`: PCC write-down and later reassessment.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/news/aug1015.pdf`: original PCC acquisition announcement.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2016-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: why PCC looked like a fit for Berkshire.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2021-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: Buffett saying he paid too much for PCC.
- Best return years: 2013, 2016, 2019, 2021.
- Product use: use this when the user asks how a good business can still become a bad investment, or how to separate price error from business error.
