# Mistake Case Dossiers

This file is not a black-history display. It is here so the Buffett mentor can talk about thesis failure, management misjudgment, and overpaying in a way that feels like Buffett rather than a slogan machine.
The research value is not that he made mistakes. The research value is how he described them, what layer was wrong, and how the doctrine changed afterward.

## Dexter Shoe
- What happened: Berkshire used Berkshire stock to buy Dexter Shoe in 1993; the business later lost the durable edge Buffett thought it had.
- What he got right at the time: management quality was not fake, and the company was not garbage on arrival.
- What he actually got wrong: he mistook good people plus past performance for a durable moat; using Berkshire equity magnified the opportunity cost.
- How to defend against it now: separate management quality from industry economics; be far stricter when paying with exceptional equity.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/1999ar/1999ar.pdf`: Dexter under pressure from import competition.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2007ltr.pdf`: Buffett later calling Dexter his worst deal.

## Tesco
- What happened: Berkshire owned 415 million Tesco shares at the end of 2012, grew uneasy in 2013, but sold too slowly and fully exited only after the damage was clearer in 2014.
- What he got right at the time: Tesco had scale, position, and had been a leading UK food retailer.
- What he actually got wrong: he reacted too slowly to management and business deterioration; dawdling replaced thesis discipline.
- How to defend against it now: when bad news turns serial and involves management, accounting, and share loss, do not let "wait and see" replace a thesis rewrite.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2014ar/2014ar.pdf`: Buffett writing about dawdling, the after-tax loss, and the serial bad-news pattern.

## Kraft Heinz
- What happened: Berkshire helped structure Heinz in 2013, but the later Kraft Heinz combination ran into major brand and operating pressure, with heavy impairment signals in 2018-2019.
- What he got right at the time: the asset quality, cash generation, and structure design were not imaginary.
- What he actually got wrong: he overstated brand durability and the power of cost takeout relative to changing consumer behavior and reinvestment needs.
- How to defend against it now: a brand is not a moat by itself; if growth, pricing power, and channel position weaken together, the thesis cannot hide behind brand prestige.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2013ar/2013ar.pdf`: Heinz transaction structure inside Berkshire.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2019ar/2019ar.pdf`: Kraft Heinz impairment and Berkshire's reevaluation context.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2020ar/2020ar.pdf`: continued economic consequence of the Kraft Heinz mistake.

## Precision Castparts
- What happened: Berkshire announced PCC in 2015, closed it in 2016, took a very large write-down after the pandemic shock, and Buffett later said he paid too much.
- What he got right at the time: PCC was a real high-grade aerospace supplier with real customer relationships and capability.
- What he actually got wrong: he was too optimistic about recovery, end-market durability, and price paid; the error was fundamentally an overpayment error.
- How to defend against it now: a good business can still be a bad investment; when the industry is cyclical and shock-prone, demand a wider valuation margin.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/news/aug1015.pdf`: original acquisition announcement and positive framing.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2016-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: why PCC looked right at the time.
  - `https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/2021ar/2021ar.pdf`: formal write-down consequence.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2021-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: Buffett saying he paid too much.

## USAir / airline lesson
- What happened: the old USAir preferred investment did not become the timeless success Buffett once hoped, and the longer lesson was that airline economics remain structurally hard; even after re-entering airlines later, Berkshire exited them in 2020.
- What he got right at the time: at certain moments, security structure, price, and managerial improvement can make an airline investment look attractive.
- What he actually got wrong: better management does not equal better industry economics; airlines remain exposed to capital intensity, supply discipline failure, and external shocks.
- How to defend against it now: do not treat good management as a cure for a structurally difficult industry; where economics can be reset by outside shocks, require a far larger margin of safety.
- First-hand source anchors:
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/2017-berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting/`: Buffett explaining why he bought airline stocks again after calling airlines a death trap.
  - `https://buffett.cnbc.com/video/2020/05/20/berkshire-hathaway-annual-meeting-2020---part-1.html`: Buffett admitting the airline-business call was wrong and explaining the exit.
